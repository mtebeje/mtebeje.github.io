The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) manages the largest immigration detention system in the world and spends more on immigration enforcement than on all other federal enforcement agencies combined.

Gov. Greg Abbott signs three bills into law at a border wall construction site in Brownsville, Texas on Monday, Dec. 18, 2023, that will broaden his border security plans and add funding for more infrastructure to deter illegal immigration. (AP Photo/Valerie Gonzalez)

Yesterday sparking controversy Governor Abbott of Texas signed a new law, known as SB4, which will allow Texas law enforcement the ability to arrest and jail migrants. It will also allow state judges to issue immediate deportation orders.

According to Immigration and Customs Enforcement they reported over 36,000 individuals are currently in ICE detention according to data as recently as December 3, 2023. Of those individuals almost 68% have no criminal record and a majority of migrants have been held due to minor offenses, including traffic violations. In November alone ICE and CBP detained and booked over 17,000 people.

The ACLU reported as of July 2023, ICE detains on average 30,003 people each day. This is a significant increase from the start of the Biden administration in January 2021, when ICE held an average of 15,444 people in detention each day. Although the number of detained individuals have decreased in recent years there is a rise in Alternatives to Detention (ATD) programs that keep migrants under survalliance.

According to ICE data, over 90 percent of people are held in privately-run immigrant detention centers. And although the number of detain migrants are slowly decreasing ICE and private prison companies are continuing to expand electronic monitoring. ICE Alternatives to Detention (ATD) or ecarceration programs are currently monitoring over 190,000 families and single individuals as reported by ICE.
